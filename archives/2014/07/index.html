Hindsight bias

The definition of this word in Davis Myers’s book Social Psychology is:

The tendency to exaggerate, after learing an outcome, one’s ability to have forseen how something turned out.

I think this bias exists more or less in people’s mind, because it’s very easy to look backwards when connecting dots rather than look forwards.
As the Danish philosopher–theologian Søren Kierkegaard put it, “Life is lived forwards, but understood backwards.”
Personally speaking, when I read the deduction procedure of some algorithm I may find the materials seems easy. But if only provided with the requirement of a problem, it’s very hard for me to figure out a fine algorithm. This example may not be persvasive, but the truth behind it, I believe, is that we find it easy to connecting dots looking backwards, but don’t have a clue when looking forwards.
This bias reminds me of some studying methods that I have ignored. In the past, I often went to the extent of understand why the textbook said was right, but didn’t start from the beginning to choose the right path. If I have done it more often, I would become more familiar with those knowledge.
Just as I may ignore the fasinating part of discovering some knowledge, we may sometimes also unable to see people’s effort devoted into some works. Because most time, we can appreciate those works, but we can’t do it. That is the effort hidden behind the final result. If we want to reaching the same height as some great “mountain climbers”, we must put ourself at the foot of the mountain and ask ourself, “how can you climb to the top?”
